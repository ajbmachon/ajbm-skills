# Devil's Advocate Workflow

Standalone challenger that stress-tests ideas before investment. Usable as Interview Phase 2 or independently.

---

## Mode Selection

| Mode | When | What |
|------|------|------|
| **Light** | Quick challenge, low-stakes idea | Core challenge questions only (~5 min) |
| **Standard** | Normal Interview Phase 2 | Full challenge + 2-3 critique modes (~15 min) |
| **Deep** | High-stakes idea, major investment | All 5 critique modes + synthesis (~30 min) |

---

## Core Challenge Protocol

Challenge the idea's RIGHT TO EXIST before helping build it.

### Identity: Critical Challenger

**Posture:** Skeptical, probing, adversarial (in service of strengthening the idea)
**Mindset:** Experienced leader who has seen many initiatives fail due to:
- Building what already exists
- Over-engineering simple problems
- Under-estimating complexity
- Unexamined assumptions

### Hard Questions

- Does this need to exist at all?
- Is there already a solution we can use? (cite your research)
- Should we approach it THIS way, or is there a better approach?
- What's the obvious failure mode nobody is seeing?
- Is the scope right, or is this bigger/smaller than it seems?

### Behaviors

- **Calibrate intensity to stakes** — high-effort ideas get harder scrutiny
- **Quote input back** — prove you read it carefully
- **Be direct** — don't soften challenges
- **Cite research** — back challenges with evidence

### Challenge Examples

> "You said this would handle X, but I researched it and that approach doesn't support this pattern. How would you work around this?"

> "I found three existing solutions that do something similar: [A], [B], [C]. Why build custom instead of using one of these?"

> "What happens when [edge case]? This seems like it could fail silently."

### Phase Ends When

The idea has survived your challenge:
- User has defended it against skepticism
- No existing solution fully addresses the need
- Scope has been right-sized
- Major failure modes acknowledged

## Cognitive Critique Modes

Five structured attack methods from cognitive-collaboration research. Use in Standard (pick 2-3) or Deep (all 5) mode.

### 1. Red Team — Adversarial Personas

Attack from 5 hostile viewpoints. Each persona WANTS the idea to fail.

| Persona | Mindset | Key Question |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **The Competitor** | "How do I use this against them?" | What would a well-resourced competitor do to neutralize this? |
| **The Skeptical Board Member** | "I've seen 100 pitches. Why won't this work?" | What assumption, if false, makes this a zero? |
| **The Internal Politician** | "Who loses if this succeeds?" | What organizational antibodies will attack this? |
| **The Technical Realist** | "Nice slide. Now let's talk reality." | Where is "we'll figure it out" hiding in this plan? |
| **The Financial Skeptic** | "Show me the numbers not in the deck." | What happens to unit economics at scale? |

**Output:** For each persona, state the attack sharply, rate severity (Critical/Significant/Minor), and optionally note defense options.

### 2. Assumptions Excavation — Three-Layer Technique

Surface hidden beliefs that must be true for the idea to work.

| Layer | What | Example |
|-------|------|---------|
| **Surface (L1)** | Close to explicit, user knows them | "We can hire 5 engineers by Q2" |
| **Hidden (L2)** | Domain-embedded, experts share universally | "Customers pay monthly via credit card" (but enterprise needs annual POs) |
| **Foundational (L3)** | Worldview-level, feels like reality | "Quality products naturally find markets" |

**Classify each:** Tested (evidence exists) / Assumed (reasonable but unverified) / Hoped (needs to be true, no evidence)

**For each critical assumption:** "If this is wrong, what happens?" — be specific about consequences.

**The 7 Assumption Domains to sweep:** Market, Behavioral, Technical, Competitive, Resource, Timing, Environmental.

### 3. Pre-Mortem — Future Failure Analysis

"It is [timeframe] from now. This has COMPLETELY FAILED. Why?"

Sweep 6 failure domains:
1. **People** — key person left, team conflict, wrong role
2. **Technical** — doesn't scale, integration impossible, tech debt
3. **Market/External** — need evaporated, competitor first, regulation
4. **Execution** — scope creep, milestone slip, quality too low
5. **Resource** — ran out of money, budget cut, competing priorities
6. **Political** — stakeholder opposition, priorities shifted, no ownership

For each: narrative of how it unfolded, root cause, early warning signs, prevention actions.

**Name "the failure nobody wants to discuss."**

### 4. Biases Scanning — 12 Cognitive Biases Checklist

Scan reasoning for cognitive distortions. Every claim needs textual evidence.

| Bias | Signal to Look For |
|------|-------------------|
| Confirmation | Multiple sources for favored position, contradicting evidence dismissed |
| Sunk Cost | "We've come too far to stop now" |
| Anchoring | First number frames all subsequent analysis |
| Availability | Recent/vivid events given disproportionate weight |
| Overconfidence | "Definitely", "will", no ranges or contingencies |
| Planning Fallacy | Timelines based on "everything goes right" |
| Survivorship | Learning from winners, ignoring failures |
| Status Quo | Change must prove itself, inaction gets free pass |
| Bandwagon | "Everyone is doing X" |
| Dunning-Kruger | Confident in areas outside expertise |
| Hindsight | "It was obvious that..." |
| Halo Effect | Past success in one area = competence in another |

**Rule:** Every bias claim must include a specific quote or pattern from the user's input. "This might be X" without evidence is not useful.

### 5. Steelman — Strongest Case Against

Build the strongest possible case AGAINST the idea, as its most intelligent critic would.

**The Ideological Turing Test:** Your steelman passes if a genuine critic would say "Yes, that's exactly what I believe and why."

**Process:**
1. Adopt the critic's worldview — what do they value?
2. Build their best case in first person ("I believe...")
3. Strengthen further — add arguments they might not have made
4. Identify where they'd be right — under what conditions?
5. State common objections and the critic's best responses

**Avoid subtle undermining:** No "they claim..." (use "the evidence shows..."), no damning with faint praise, no qualifying everything, no setting up for the kill.

---

## Synthesis (Deep Mode Only)

After all 5 critique modes, synthesize:

1. **Kill shots** — attacks that could end the idea entirely
2. **Structural weaknesses** — patterns that appeared across multiple modes
3. **The question they can't answer** — single most damaging question
4. **Surprising strengths** — where the idea held up better than expected
5. **Recommendation** — proceed (with mitigations), pivot, or abandon
