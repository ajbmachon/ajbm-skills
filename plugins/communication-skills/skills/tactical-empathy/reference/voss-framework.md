# Chris Voss Negotiation Framework — Activation Reference

Loaded on demand during Analyze or complex Spar. Activates precise recall — you already know these techniques from training. This file provides the specific cues, phrasing patterns, and failure modes that make the difference between generic recall and expert-level application.

---

## The Big Three (Primary Weapons)

### Mirroring (Isopraxism)

Repeat last 1-3 critical words, upward inflection, then silence (4+ seconds). The silence is where it works — a void the human brain is compelled to fill. Mirror neuron activation makes their brain treat you as "same tribe," lowering defenses.

**Selective mirroring steers the conversation:** "We can't do anything about the price right now" →
- "Right now?" → steers to timing
- "The price?" → steers to flexibility
- "Can't do anything?" → steers to constraints

Choose which thread to pull. This is what makes mirroring strategic, not mechanical.

**Mirror + silence = soft "no."** When someone makes an unreasonable demand, mirror it back and wait. Devastatingly effective without confrontation.

**Stack mirrors** to peel layers. Each mirror in a sequence goes deeper. Default to mirroring when you don't know what else to do — it buys time, gathers information, and builds connection simultaneously.

**Pitfalls:**
- Paraphrasing instead of exact words (breaks the neurological connection — their words, not yours)
- Filling the silence after the mirror (kills the technique — the discomfort IS the tool)
- Mirroring too many words (1-3 max, precision matters)

**Phrasing pattern:**
```
Them: "[statement with revealing words]"
You: "[1-3 of their words]?" [upward inflection, then 4+ seconds silence]
```

### Labeling

"It seems like..." / "It sounds like..." / "It looks like..." — never start with "I." Name the emotion you detect, stated or unstated, then go silent. Confirmation deepens trust; correction gives you better information. Both outcomes are wins.

**Why precision matters:** Naming an emotion moves it from amygdala (reactive, hijacking) to prefrontal cortex (aware, manageable). Vague labels don't create this shift because they're not specific enough to trigger recognition.

**Precision spectrum:**
```
Useless:  "It seems like you have concerns."
Weak:     "It seems like you're frustrated."
Good:     "It seems like you're frustrated about the timeline change."
Surgical: "It seems like you feel this timeline change doesn't respect
           the three months your team already invested in the original plan."
```

The surgical label is the one that gets "that's right." Every word matters.

**Advanced applications:**
- **Label positives** to reinforce: "It seems like your team is really excited about the Q2 vision" — builds alliance
- **Label the dynamic**, not just the emotion: "It seems like every time we get close to a number, something pulls you back" — names the pattern
- **Pre-emptive labeling** (Accusation Audit overlap): label the emotion before they have it: "You're probably going to feel like this ask is unreasonable..."
- **Label to create empathy FOR you:** "It seems like you can see this puts me in a really difficult position" — invites their empathy without asking
- **Stack labels** when one lands: peel the onion deeper after "that's right"

**Pitfalls:**
- Starting with "What I'm hearing is..." (makes it about you, not them)
- Vague labels — nearly worthless, don't create recognition
- Following a label with your own point instead of waiting for their response
- Only labeling negatives — positive labeling builds alliance
- Labeling what you THINK they feel vs what evidence suggests

**Phrasing patterns:**
```
"It seems like [specific emotion about specific issue]..."
"It sounds like [what's really at stake for them]..."
"It looks like [the dynamic you observe]..."
```

### Calibrated Questions — Saying No Without Saying No

"How" and "What" questions make your problem their problem. A direct "no" creates adversaries; a calibrated question maintains collaboration while forcing them to confront reality. They confront the impossibility of their own demand without you arguing against it.

**The deeper principle:** The person who defines the questions controls the conversation. They think they're driving because they're answering. The questions are steering.

**The master question:** "How am I supposed to do that?" — gentle, devastating "no." Use when someone makes a demand that ignores your constraints.

**Essential arsenal:**
- "What about this is important to you?" — reveals real priorities (often different from stated position)
- "How does this affect the rest of the team?" — forces wider-impact thinking
- "What happens if we do nothing?" — tests if urgency is real or manufactured
- "How would you like me to proceed?" — gives control while committing them to a direction
- "What are we trying to accomplish here?" — resets to shared purpose when things go sideways
- "How can I help make this better for us?" — cooperative frame
- "What's the biggest challenge you're facing?" — invites constraint sharing (Black Swan hunting)

**Advanced:**
- **Implementation question:** "How does this work?" forces them to think through details of their demand — they often realize it won't work as stated
- **Constraint surfacer:** "What constraints are you working with?" gives permission to share hidden limitations
- **Future question:** "What happens next?" forces thinking beyond immediate negotiation to consequences
- **Stacked:** After one answer, follow deeper: "What does success look like?" → "How do you see us getting there together?"

**Rules:**
- **Never "why"** — sounds accusatory, triggers defensiveness. "What caused this?" gets the same information safely.
- **Never closed questions** — "Can you do better?" → "No." Dead end. "How can we work together on this?" → problem-solving.
- **Max 3 in sequence** — more feels like interrogation
- **Listen to the answer** — it reveals constraints, priorities, Black Swans

**Phrasing pattern:**
```
Them: "[unreasonable demand or position]"
You: "How am I supposed to [their demand restated as your constraint]?"
— or —
You: "What does [their demand] look like from [affected party's] side?"
```

## Full Arsenal

### Tactical Empathy
The foundation, not a technique. Every interaction filtered through: "What are they feeling and why?" Understanding their perspective to influence behavior. Not sympathy (feeling sorry). Not agreement (conceding). Understanding their worldview as your operating map.

### Accusation Audit
Proactively name every negative thing they might think about you BEFORE they say it. "You're probably thinking..." / "This is going to sound..." Be specific and unflinching — vague audits don't work. Overdo it slightly; they'll say "it's not that bad" and soften. Don't follow with "but." Let the audit land.

Use at the opening of any negotiation with likely objections. Before bad news. Before big asks.

### No-Oriented Questions
Let them say "no" — it gives control and safety. "Would it be ridiculous to...?" "Is it a bad idea if...?" "Have you given up on...?" Their "no" = your "yes." People are more comfortable with "no" (protection) than "yes" (commitment). Three consecutive "yes" questions trigger counterfeit agreement — switch to no-oriented.

### "That's Right" vs "You're Right"
**"That's right"** = genuine breakthrough. The counterpart feels fully understood. Earned through accurate summary: paraphrase their meaning + label the emotion underneath. Let it stand without pivoting to your ask.

**"You're right"** = dismissal. They want to end the conversation. Comes quick, no reflection, followed by silence or topic change. Never accept it as victory. If you hear "you're right," your summary wasn't good enough — go deeper.

### Late-Night FM DJ Voice
Calm, deliberate, downward-inflecting tone. De-escalates tension, builds rapport, signals confidence. One of three Voss voices: positive/playful (default mode), late-night FM DJ (de-escalation, difficult moments), assertive (rare — only when rapport is deep and the moment demands directness).

### Tactical Silence
The cheapest, most underused tool. Deploy after every mirror, label, and calibrated question. Minimum 4 seconds. The discomfort you feel is the technique working. Silence creates a void people are compelled to fill — usually with more truth than any question would extract.

### Black Swan Discovery
Hidden information that, if discovered, completely changes the negotiation dynamic. Unknown unknowns — distinct from Taleb's statistical concept. Every negotiation has 1-3 Black Swans.

**How to hunt:** Questions that create space for unexpected answers. Listening to offhand comments and corrections. Paying attention before/after formal meetings. Face-to-face reveals more than calls or email. Emotional reactions disproportionate to the topic signal a Black Swan underneath.

### Bend Reality (Loss Framing + Anchoring)
**Loss framing:** Show what they LOSE by not agreeing — 2x more powerful than showing gains (loss aversion). "If we don't act by Friday, the other team takes the slot."

**Extreme anchoring:** Set a high/low anchor early so your real ask feels reasonable by comparison. Anchor with confidence, combine with empathy or it reads as a threat. Remember: anchoring works on you too — recognize when they anchor you.

### Ackerman Bargaining Model
Systematic: 65% → 85% → 95% → 100% of your target. Each increment decreases, signaling you're approaching your limit. Use empathy and calibrated questions between each raise — not mechanical jumping.

**Final offer specifics:** Non-round number ($37,893 not $38,000) — precision signals thorough calculation. Include a non-monetary sweetener (something they probably don't even want) — signals you're at your absolute limit.

### Rule of Three
Three agreements to the same thing, different approaches each time:
1. Their initial "yes" or "that's right"
2. Summarize what they agreed to, get confirmation
3. Calibrated "how" question about implementation

If they can't agree three times, the deal isn't real — people find it very hard to fake genuine agreement three times. Vary your approach to avoid being obvious.

### Negotiator Types

| Type | Drives | How to Negotiate With Them |
|------|--------|--------------------------|
| **Analyst** | Data, preparation, minimizing mistakes | Give them time to process. Use data anchors. Don't rush. They love silence — don't misread it as disagreement. They distrust anything improvised. |
| **Accommodator** | Relationship, rapport, harmony | Build rapport before business. Labels land well — they respond to feeling heard. Beware counterfeit "yes" — they agree to preserve relationship. Use Rule of Three to verify genuine agreement. |
| **Assertive** | Efficiency, being heard, time = money | Let them talk first — they won't listen until they feel heard. Mirror aggressively (shows you're paying attention). Once heard, they'll give you their attention. Don't waste their time. |

**Self-assessment:** Prepare obsessively? Analyst. Prioritize relationship? Accommodator. Want to get to the point? Assertive.

**Your blind spot is your vulnerability:** Analysts over-prepare and miss emotional cues. Accommodators agree too easily. Assertives bulldoze and miss information.
