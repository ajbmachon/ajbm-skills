# Complement Frameworks — Deep Reference

Four surgical complements loaded when the situation requires tools outside Voss's core coverage. Each fills a specific gap in the Voss framework.

---

## BATNA Analysis (Fisher/Ury — "Getting to Yes")

**Gap filled:** Voss focuses on the conversation itself but has weak preparation structure for understanding walkaway positions and negotiation zones.

**Phase:** Prepare (before the conversation)

### Core Concept

BATNA = Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement. Your power in any negotiation comes from your ability to walk away. The better your alternative, the stronger your position.

### How to Calculate

**Your BATNA:**
1. List every alternative if this negotiation fails
2. Evaluate each alternative realistically (not optimistically)
3. Select the best one — that's your BATNA
4. Improve it if possible before negotiating (this is often the highest-leverage prep)

**Their BATNA:**
1. What happens to them if you walk away?
2. What alternatives do they have?
3. How good are those alternatives?
4. The worse their BATNA, the more they need this deal

**ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement):**
- Your reservation point: the worst deal you'd accept (just barely better than your BATNA)
- Their reservation point: the worst they'd accept
- If these overlap → ZOPA exists → deal is possible
- If no overlap → no deal is possible at current terms → change the terms or walk away

### Integration with Voss

Use BATNA analysis in the Prepare phase, then deploy Voss techniques (accusation audit, calibrated questions, Ackerman) during the conversation. BATNA tells you *when* to walk away. Voss techniques tell you *how to stay and negotiate*.

## OFNR Sequence (Nonviolent Communication — Marshall Rosenberg)

**Gap filled:** Voss is almost entirely about listening and influencing the other side. OFNR provides structure for clearly expressing your own position without triggering defensiveness.

**Phase:** Speak (when you need to state your needs)

### Core Concept

OFNR = Observation → Feeling → Need → Request. A four-step formula for expressing yourself clearly without blame or judgment.

### The Sequence

**O — Observation (fact, not judgment):**
- What you concretely observe, without evaluation
- "When the project timeline was moved up by three weeks..." (not "When you unfairly changed the deadline...")

**F — Feeling (emotion, not thought):**
- What you feel, not what you think about their behavior
- "...I feel concerned..." (not "I feel like you don't respect my time" — that's a thought disguised as a feeling)

**N — Need (universal human need, not strategy):**
- The underlying need driving the feeling
- "...because I need predictability to deliver quality work..."

**R — Request (specific, actionable, refusable):**
- A concrete action you're requesting — NOT a demand
- "...would you be willing to discuss the new timeline before it's finalized?"

### Complete Example

```
"When I see the contract includes a 90-day payment term [O],
 I feel uneasy [F] because I need financial stability to keep
 the team focused on your project [N]. Would you be open to
 discussing a 30-day term with a milestone structure? [R]"
```

### Integration with Voss

Use OFNR when you need to express your position clearly. Then return to Voss techniques (labels, calibrated questions) to handle their response. The combination: OFNR for speaking, Voss for listening and influencing.

## Safety Monitoring (Crucial Conversations — Patterson, Grenny, McMillan, Switzler)

**Gap filled:** Voss has no explicit tools for when conversations completely break down — when the counterpart goes silent (withdrawal) or becomes verbally aggressive (attack). Safety Monitoring provides the repair mechanism.

**Phase:** Repair (when the conversation breaks down)

### Core Concept

When people feel unsafe, they resort to either **silence** (masking, avoiding, withdrawing) or **violence** (controlling, labeling, attacking). Content problems can't be solved until safety is restored.

### Detecting Breakdown

**Signs of Silence:**
- Masking: sarcasm, sugarcoating, selective sharing
- Avoiding: steering away from sensitive topics
- Withdrawing: pulling out of the conversation entirely

**Signs of Violence:**
- Controlling: cutting off, overstating facts, directive language
- Labeling: putting a negative label on people or ideas to dismiss them
- Attacking: belittling, making threats

### Restoring Safety

**Step 1: Step out of content.** Stop discussing the topic. Address the safety issue.

**Step 2: Establish Mutual Purpose.**
- "I don't want [the thing they fear]. What I do want is [your real purpose]."
- "I think we both want [shared goal]. Can we figure out how to get there?"

**Step 3: Contrasting** (if they've misunderstood your intent):
- "I don't want you to think [misunderstanding]. I do want [actual intent]."
- The don't/do structure corrects the misunderstanding without being defensive.

**Step 4: Return to content** only after safety is restored.

### Complete Example

```
Them: [stands up] "This is pointless. You're just going to
       do whatever you want anyway."

You: [Step out of content] "Hold on — I can see this isn't
      working the way I intended."
     [Contrasting] "I don't want you to think your input
      doesn't matter here. I genuinely need your perspective
      to make this work."
     [Mutual Purpose] "I think we both want a solution that
      doesn't overload your team. Can we start from there?"
```

### Integration with Voss

When Voss techniques (labels, mirrors) aren't getting through because the counterpart has shut down or escalated, switch to Safety Monitoring. Restore safety first, then resume Voss techniques. Think of it as the "circuit breaker" when emotional intensity exceeds the threshold for rational engagement.

## Three Conversations Model (Difficult Conversations — Stone, Patton, Heen)

**Gap filled:** Voss works at the tactical level but doesn't explicitly address why some conversations trigger disproportionate emotional reactions. The Three Conversations model reveals the hidden layers.

**Phase:** Listen (when emotions seem disproportionate to the surface issue)

### Core Concept

Every difficult conversation is actually three conversations happening simultaneously:

### 1. The "What Happened" Conversation
- Surface level: disagreement about facts, actions, blame
- **Shift:** From certainty to curiosity. From blame to contribution.
- Instead of "Who's right?" → "How did we each contribute to this situation?"

### 2. The Feelings Conversation
- Emotional layer: unexpressed feelings driving the interaction
- **Shift:** From suppressing/weaponizing emotions → acknowledging them as legitimate data
- Feelings that aren't addressed don't go away — they leak out as sarcasm, withdrawal, or aggression

### 3. The Identity Conversation
- Deepest layer: what this situation says about who I am
- Three identity questions people wrestle with:
  - "Am I competent?" (fear of being exposed as incompetent)
  - "Am I a good person?" (fear of being seen as selfish/wrong)
  - "Am I worthy of love/respect?" (fear of rejection)
- **When identity is threatened, reactions become disproportionate.** A simple feedback conversation becomes existential because it threatens "Am I competent?"

### Detecting the Identity Layer

- Their reaction is bigger than the situation warrants
- They become defensive about things that weren't attacked
- They catastrophize ("This changes everything")
- They personalize ("You always do this to me")

### Responding to the Identity Layer

- Name it with a label: "It seems like this might feel like it's about more than just the project timeline"
- Create safety: "Nothing about this changes how I see your capability"
- Separate impact from intent: "I know you didn't mean for this to happen, and it did have this impact"

### Integration with Voss

Use Voss's labels and calibrated questions to surface the identity layer. When you detect disproportionate reactions, shift from the "What Happened" conversation to the Feelings or Identity conversation. Label the deeper emotion. Then return to the surface issue with the emotional charge reduced.
